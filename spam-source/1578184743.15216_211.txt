The areas of the taiga inside the Arctic Circle have midnight sun in mid-summer and polar night in mid-winter. Lakes and other water bodies are common in the taiga. The Helvetinj&auml;rvi National Park, Finland, is situated in the closed canopy taiga (mid-boreal to south-boreal) with mean annual temperature of 4 &deg;C (39 &deg;F). The taiga experiences relatively low precipitation throughout the year (generally 200&ndash;750 mm (7.9&ndash;29.5 in) annually, 1,000 mm (39 in) in some areas), primarily as rain during the summer months, but also as fog and snow. This fog, especially predominant in low-lying areas during and after the thawing of frozen Arctic seas, means that sunshine is not abundant in the taiga even during the long summer days. As evaporation is consequently low for most of the year, precipitation exceeds evaporation, and is sufficient to sustain the dense vegetation growth including large trees. (In the steppe biome, often found south of taiga in the northern hemisphere, evapotranspiration exceeds precipitation, restricting vegetation to mostly grasses.) Snow may remain on the ground for as long as nine months in the northernmost extensions of the taiga ecozone. In general, taiga grows to the south of the 10 &deg;C (50 &deg;F) July isotherm, but occasionally as far north as the 9 &deg;C (48 &deg;F) July isotherm. Rich in spruces, Scots pines in the western Siberian plain, the taiga is dominated by larch in Eastern Siberia, before returning to its original floristic richness on the Pacific shores. Two deciduous trees mingle throughout southern Siberia: birch and Populus tremula.</span></body>
